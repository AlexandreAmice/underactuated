\begin{align*} \min_{\bx[\cdot]} \quad & \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \| \bx[n+1] - \bx[n] \|^2, \\ \subjto \quad & \| \bx[n] - \bp_i \|^2 \ge 1, \quad & \forall i,n \\ & \bx[0] = \bx_0, \quad \bx[N] = \bx_g. \end{align*}

\begin{align*} \min_{\bx[\cdot], {\bf Y}} \quad & \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \tr({\bf Y}_{n+1,n+1}) - 2\tr({\bf Y}_{n,n+1}) + \tr({\bf Y}_{n,n}) \\ \subjto \quad & {\bf Y} \succeq \bx\bx^T \\ & \tr({\bf Y}_{n,n}) - 2 \bp_i^T\bx[n] + \bp_i^T\bp_i \ge 1, \quad & \forall i,n \\ & \bx[0] = \bx_0, \quad \bx[N] = \bx_g. \end{align*} where ${\bf Y}_{i,j}$ is the block submatrix of ${\bf Y}$ corresponding to $\bx[i]\bx^T[j]$. We can write ${\bf Y} \succeq \bx\bx^T$ as a semidefinite constraint using the Schur complement: $\begin{bmatrix} {\bf Y} & \bx \\ \bx^T & 1 \end{bmatrix} \succeq 0.$ It is quite natural to extend this to general ellipsoidal obstacles. Also, very few terms in ${\bf Y}$ are actually needed for the optimization; one could write many small SDP constraints instead for efficiency.

Perhaps even more interesting, with some care we can use the same relaxation to formulate an LQR problem (or most any of our variants) with these non-convex quadratic constraints. \begin{align*} \min_{\bx[\cdot], \bu[\cdot]} \quad & \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \bx^T[n]\bQ \bx[n] + \bu^T[n] \bR \bu[n], \\ \subjto \quad & \| \bx[n] - \bp_i \|^2 \ge 1, \quad & \forall i,n \\ & \bx[n+1] = \bA \bx[n] + \bB \bu[n], \quad & \forall n \\ & \bx[0] = \bx_0, \quad \bx[N] = \bx_g. \end{align*} Another interesting application of this is LQR with binary inputs, $\bu[n] \in \{-1, +1\}$ Parrilo??.

\begin{align*} \min_{\bx[\cdot], \bu[\cdot], {\bf X}[\cdot], {\bf U}[\cdot]} \quad & \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \tr(\bR {\bf U}[n]), \\ \subjto \quad & \tr({\bf X}[n]) - 2 \bp_i^T\bx[n] + {\bf p}_i^T{\bf p}_i \ge 1, \quad & \forall i, \forall n \in [1,N-1], \\ & \bx[n+1] = \bA \bx[n] + \bB \bu[n], \quad & \forall n, \\ & \begin{bmatrix} {\bf U}[n] & \bu[n] \\ \bu^T[n] & 1 \end{bmatrix} \succeq 0, & \forall n \in [0, N-1], \\& \begin{bmatrix} {\bf X}[n] & \bx[n] \\ \bx^T[n] & 1 \end{bmatrix} \succeq 0, & \forall n\in[1,N-1], \\ & \bx[0] = \bx_0, \quad \bx[N] = \bx_g. \end{align*} It's easy enough to extend this to the case of ellipsoidal obstacles, or to add the quadratic state penalty to the cost.
